In professional animation, gaming, and comic pipelines, artists use a variety of specialized reference sheets to keep a character "on-model" (visually consistent).

When working with image generators, prompting for these specific industry formats is highly effective because AI models have been trained on vast datasets of production art.

Using specific sheet formats can help isolate your character from the background and prepare them for different scenes and storytelling needs:

---

### 1. The Action / Pose Sheet

While a turnaround sheet provides strict technical angles, an **Action Sheet** captures how your character moves, bends, and holds themselves. It shows full-body dynamic gestures (e.g., running, crouching, sitting, fighting) that match their personality.

* **Why use it:** Generating this sheet gives you a library of the character's entire body in motion on a clean canvas, which you can easily cut out and place into storyboards.
* **Prompt Keyword Formula:** > `[Character Description], action pose sheet, character poses, dynamic gestures, multiple full-body poses, standing, running, sitting, isolated on a solid white background`

### 2. The Construction / Anatomy Sheet

In traditional animation, a **Construction Sheet** breaks down the character into simple geometric primitives (spheres, cylinders, cubes) and establishes height proportions (e.g., "7 heads tall").

* **Why use it:** If an AI model is struggling to understand your character's skeletal structure or is distorting their limbs across different angles, this sheet forces a clean, technical blueprint layout.
* **Prompt Keyword Formula:** > `[Character Description], character construction sheet, anatomy blueprint, proportion guide, wireframe guidelines, technical drawing, structural breakdown, flat colors, plain white background`

### 3. The Color Reference / Texture Sheet

A **Color Sheet** sets the exact palette for the production team. It displays the character in a flat, unshaded (neutral) light with color swatches or callouts for their hair, skin, and clothing.

* **Why use it:** AI loves to randomly add complex dramatic shadows or shift a character's clothing colors from scene to scene. Prompting for a flat color sheet locks in the absolute base textures without any environmental lighting pollution.
* **Prompt Keyword Formula:** > `[Character Description], color sheet, character reference palette, flat colors, no shadows, neutral studio lighting, color swatches, front and side view, clean white background`

### 4. The Wardrobe / Costume Change Sheet

A **Wardrobe Sheet** displays the exact same character standing in identical poses, but wearing different outfits (e.g., casual clothes, battle armor, winter gear, formal attire).

* **Why use it:** If your storyboard spans multiple days, locations, or time periods, you need to change your character's clothes without changing their face and body identity.
* **Prompt Keyword Formula:** > `[Character Description], wardrobe sheet, costume design matrix, multiple outfit variations, concept art sheet, same character wearing different clothes, isolated on plain white background`

### 5. The Model Turnaround Sheet (The Foundation)

As a quick reminder, this is the classic production sheet showing a single character standing in a neutral "T-pose" or relaxed state, rotated incrementally.

* **Angles included:** Front view, 3/4 view (diagonal), side profile, and back view.
* **Prompt Keyword Formula:** > `[Character Description], character turnaround sheet, model sheet, front view, side view, back view, three-quarter view, uniform alignment, solid white background`

---

### Pro-Tips for Generating Sheets with AI:

1. **Enforce "Uniform Alignment":** Use the phrase `aligned on a continuous grid line` or `linear sequence`. This discourages the AI from scattering the poses randomly across the image and forces them onto a flat, extractable horizontal plane.
2. **Combine Expressions and Poses:** If you want a comprehensive sheet, you can prompt for a **"Model Sheet Bundle"**:
> `[Character Description], comprehensive character asset sheet, including full-body turnaround angles and close-up facial expression sheet, isolated layout, solid white background`


3. **Stick to "Flat Colors" or "2D Concept Art Style":** Even if your ultimate goal is a highly cinematic scene, generating your initial asset sheets in a clean `2D vector art`, `clean line art`, or `flat anime color` style makes background removal dramatically cleaner. It is significantly easier to add dramatic cinematic lighting later once the character is placed inside a scene.
